# Ireland — T&A requirements

> **What this is.** The ground-truth reference for Ireland's time-&-attendance legal requirements,
> detailed enough to **build a day.io pay policy from**. It aims to be **exhaustive** and **atomic**:
> **one legal proposition per row**, each row self-contained (no "see §X" as the only content), with
> exact values, a worked example wherever a number is involved, variants, and a `Basis` that **links
> to the primary source** so any row can be checked against the law directly.
>
> **Scope: time & day-events only** — money (%, €, gross-to-net) is downstream *context* here (kept
> in `Values` so a policy can be configured; the deliverable is the typed hour/day event).
> **Irish-term convention:** every non-English (or Ireland-specific institutional) term is glossed
> in English in brackets on first use, e.g. *Garda Síochána* [the national police force].
>
> **The one structural fact that shapes this whole document.** Ireland has **one central statute**,
> not a stacked federal/regional system like Australia's: the **Organisation of Working Time Act
> 1997** (OWTA, transposing EU Working Time Directive 2003/88/EC) sets working-time *limits* (48h
> weekly average, rest periods, breaks) much like Germany's *ArbZG* — but unlike Germany it **does**
> create statutory pay-related *entitlements* for Sunday work and public holidays, it just leaves
> their **rate undefined** ("reasonable … having regard to all the circumstances"). The operative
> **overtime premium is wholly contractual/collective — Ireland has no statutory overtime premium
> at all.** A small number of sectors (security, contract cleaning) have a **Joint Labour Committee
> (JLC)**-made **Employment Regulation Order (ERO)** — a sector-level statutory pay floor — layered
> on top of the OWTA floor; everyone else runs on contract only.
>
> **Legal sources & links.** Primary register: the **Law Reform Commission's revised Acts service**
> (`revisedacts.lawreform.ie`), which mirrors the Irish Statute Book with in-force consolidated text
> and a stable per-section URL pattern (confirmed live 2026-07-21):
> `revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/<year>/act/<no>/section/<n>/revised/en/html`. OWTA section links
> below resolve against this pattern. Statutory instruments (S.I.) and secondary statutes are linked
> to `irishstatutebook.ie` where a stable article-level link could not be independently re-confirmed
> in this pass — flagged 🔎. Regulator guidance (WRC, gov.ie) is linked for non-statutory figures.
> 🔎 marks a figure or deep link not independently re-verified this pass.

## 2. Working-time definition

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **What counts as working time** | An employee is "working" when at their **place of work, or at the employer's disposal**, and **carrying out the activities/duties of the work** (both limbs required). This single definition drives every downstream count (weekly average, night-worker status, rest-period compliance). | Genuine on-site presence performing duties counts; being merely reachable from home generally does not (see §8 for the on-call split). | Interpreted consistently with the CJEU's Working-Time-Directive case law (SIMAP, Jaeger, Matzak — see §8), since OWTA transposes the Directive. | [OWTA 1997 s.2](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/2/revised/en/html) |
| **"Night time" — the anchor window** | **Midnight–07:00** is the Act's single definition of night time, used consistently to determine night-worker status (§5), the night-work hour cap (§5), and (via the youth regime) the minors' night ban (§5). | A punch at 23:30 falls inside the night-time window; a punch at 07:30 does not. | Different sectoral night-ban windows apply to minors (§5). | [OWTA 1997 s.2](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/2/revised/en/html) |
| **Reference-period exclusions (working-time averaging)** | When averaging hours for the weekly cap (§4) or the night-work cap (§5), the reference period **excludes**: annual leave, parental/force-majeure/medical-care/domestic-violence leave, carer's leave, maternity/adoptive leave, and sick leave — so an averaging window isn't diluted by protected absence. | A worker's 4-month averaging window skips any weeks spent on maternity leave when computing the 48h average. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.15](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/15/revised/en/html)–[s.16](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/16/revised/en/html) |

## 3. Overtime

*Ireland has no statutory overtime premium and no statutory OT onset — the single most important
distinction, shared with Germany. OWTA regulates working-time **limits**; OT pay is a pure
contract/ERO matter, floored only by the national minimum wage on every hour worked.*

### 3a. Onset / trigger

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No statutory OT onset** | Onset is wholly contract/ERO-defined. Nothing in OWTA marks a threshold ("beyond 8h/day" or "beyond 40h/week") as triggering a premium. | A 39h-contract worker doing 45h has 6 "extra" hours; whether they carry any premium is purely contractual/ERO. | Common contractual onsets: "beyond the normal roster" or "beyond 39h/week" (traditional Irish full-time reference). | (none statutory); [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) sets no OT provision |
| **Part-time onset = contractual hours** | For part-timers, "extra hours" start above their **contract** hours (pro-rata principle), not a full-time line. | 20h contract, works 28h → 8 additional hours from the 21st. | — | [Protection of Employees (Part-Time Work) Act 2001](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2001/act/45/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Minimum-wage floor applies to every hour** | Whatever the contractual OT rate (including "none"), every hour — including OT hours — must clear the **National Minimum Wage**: **€14.15/hour** (age 20+) from 1 Jan 2026, with sub-minimum youth bands (19: €12.74; 18: €11.32; <18: €9.91). | An OT hour paid at "flat rate" for a 20-year-old must still clear €14.15/hour. | Sub-minimum rates apply only to workers under 20 (and some trainee categories). | [National Minimum Wage Act 2000](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2000/act/5/revised/en/html) 🔎; annual NMW Order |

### 3b. Rate & bands

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No statutory OT premium** | No legal uplift for overtime hours worked on an ordinary weekday. 🔎 Market convention commonly cites **time-and-a-half (150%)** as typical, but this is custom/contract, not law. | Silent contract → ordinary (≥ NMW) rate only for extra hours. | ERO-covered sectors (security, contract cleaning) set their own OT rates in the ERO. | (none statutory); [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) sets no OT provision |
| **Sunday and public-holiday premiums are statutory *rights*, undefined *rate*** | Unlike ordinary-day OT, Sunday work (s.14) and public-holiday work (s.21) trigger a **statutory entitlement to compensation** — but the Act leaves the **rate/form to "reasonableness"** rather than setting a %. Detailed in §6. | — | — | [OWTA 1997 s.14](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/14/revised/en/html), [s.21](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/21/revised/en/html) |

### 3c. Caps & counters

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No statutory annual OT-hours cap** | Ireland doesn't cap overtime *hours* as such — OT is constrained only by the **48-hour weekly average** ceiling (§4), which folds ordinary + OT hours into one counter. | A worker averaging 50h/week for a month is fine only if the reference-period average still nets to ≤48h. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.15](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/15/revised/en/html) |

### 3d. Averaging & annualisation

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No OT-determining averaging** | Ireland's reference-period averaging (4/6/12 months) validates the **48-hour working-time limit** (§4), not OT pay — it decides whether the *hours* were lawful, not what's *owed*. Placed in §4 per this file's convention (averaging that validates a limit, not one that decides OT pay). | A worker's hours are averaged over the standard 4-month reference period to confirm the 48h/week limit was respected — that average has no bearing on whether any of those hours carried an overtime premium. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.15](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/15/revised/en/html) (see §4) |

### 3e. Composition & stacking

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No statutory composition rule** | With no statutory OT premium and no statutory night premium (§5), there is no legislated stacking/pyramiding rule for combined premiums (e.g., a night hour on a Sunday). Composition is whatever the contract/ERO specifies. | A night hour worked on a Sunday: base + (contractual night premium, if any) + (statutory-but-unrated Sunday compensation) — the combination logic is contract-defined. | ERO-covered sectors may define explicit stacking. | (none statutory); [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) sets no composition rule |

## 4. Rest, breaks & working-time limits

*Hard limits → flag a breach, don't silently cap hours.*

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **No statutory daily-hours cap** | Unlike Germany's 8/10h daily ceiling, **OWTA sets no maximum hours in a single day** for adult workers — the day is constrained only *indirectly*, via the 11h daily-rest rule and the 48h weekly average. | A single 14h day is not itself unlawful if the ≥11h rest before/after it holds and the weekly average stays ≤48h. | Minors have a firm daily cap (below). | [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) — no equivalent daily-cap section |
| **Weekly ceiling — 48h average** | **≤48h/week averaged** over a reference period of **4 months** (standard), extendable to **6 months** for seasonal/impracticable cases, or up to **12 months** by a Labour-Court-approved collective agreement. | Five 60h weeks in a peak month are lawful only if lighter weeks pull the 4-month average to ≤48h. | Reference-period exclusions in §2 (leave types don't dilute the average). | [OWTA 1997 s.15](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/15/revised/en/html) |
| **Daily rest — 11 consecutive hours** | **≥11h** uninterrupted rest in every 24-hour period. | Shift ends 22:00 → next start no earlier than 09:00. | S.I. 21/1998 sectoral exemption (security/continuity/surge/agriculture) — compensatory rest still owed under s.6. | [OWTA 1997 s.11](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/11/revised/en/html) |
| **Weekly rest — 24 consecutive hours (normally including Sunday)** | **≥24h** consecutive rest per 7-day period, **normally to include Sunday**; the employer may instead give **two 24h periods** in the following week (48h combined) if operationally justified. | A worker denied a 24h break this week must get two 24h breaks (48h total) next week. | Technical/organisational grounds can justify a different weekly-rest timing. | [OWTA 1997 s.13](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/13/revised/en/html) |
| **Breaks during work** | **≥15 min** after 4.5h worked; **≥30 min** after 6h worked (the 30-min break **may subsume** the earlier 15-min one — not additive); an end-of-workday break does **not** satisfy the requirement. | An 8h shift needs a ≥30min break taken before the 6h mark is exceeded, not tacked on at knock-off. | Minister may raise the minimum durations by regulation. | [OWTA 1997 s.12](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/12/revised/en/html) |
| **S.I. 21/1998 sectoral exemption — compensatory rest owed** | Where ss.11–13 are dis-applied for a named continuity/surge/security/agriculture activity, the employer must give **equivalent compensatory rest**, or (only if genuinely impossible) another non-monetary compensating arrangement. | — | Doesn't apply to *special category* night workers under s.16. | [OWTA 1997 s.6](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/6/revised/en/html); [S.I. No. 21/1998](https://www.irishstatutebook.ie/eli/1998/si/21/made/en/print) |
| **Minors <16 — daily/weekly rest & hours** | **14h** daily rest; **2 days off/week**; break **≥30 min after 4h**; max **8h/day · 40h/week** in permitted work (35h/week holiday work; 40h/week approved work-experience). | A 15-year-old on a summer job can't be rostered past 8h/day even with employer/parent agreement. | Night ban and further detail in §5. | [Protection of Young Persons (Employment) Act 1996](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1996/act/16/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Minors 16–17 — daily/weekly rest & hours** | **12h** daily rest; **2 days off/week**; break **≥30 min after 4.5h**; max **8h/day · 40h/week**. | A 17-year-old apprentice is capped at 8h/day regardless of an adult ERO allowing longer shifts. | — | [Protection of Young Persons (Employment) Act 1996](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1996/act/16/revised/en/html) 🔎 |

## 5. Night & shift work

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Night-time window & night-worker status** | Night time = **midnight–07:00**. A **night worker** = normally works **≥3h** of daily working time in that window, **or** works **≥50%** of annual hours within it. | A rotating-shift worker regularly on 23:00–07:00 (≥3h inside midnight–07:00) qualifies as a night worker. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.2](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/2/revised/en/html) |
| **Night-worker cap — 8h average** | Night workers limited to **average 8h/24h**, over a reference period **not exceeding 2 months** (extendable by Labour-Court-approved collective agreement). | A night worker's 24-hour-average is checked over a rolling ≤2-month window, not per-shift. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.16](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/16/revised/en/html) |
| **Special-category night worker — hard 8h/day cap** | Night workers whose work involves **special hazards or heavy physical/mental strain** (identified via employer risk assessment) face a **firm 8h/day** cap — **no averaging** permitted, and the **S.I. 21/1998 sectoral exemption doesn't apply** to this category. | A night-shift worker on hazardous machinery can't have their 8h cap averaged up in a busy week. | Employer must risk-assess under Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act regulations to identify this category. | [OWTA 1997 s.16](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/16/revised/en/html); [Safety, Health and Welfare at Work Act 2005](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2005/act/10/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **No statutory night premium %** | No legal uplift for night hours. 🔎 No confirmed statutory or reliable market-benchmark % identified in this research pass — purely contract/ERO. | — | ERO-covered sectors (security, contract cleaning) set their own night premiums. | (none statutory); [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) sets no night-premium provision |

## 6. Weekend & public-holiday work

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Public holidays — 10/year, national calendar** | **New Year's Day** (1 Jan) · **St Brigid's Day** (1st Mon in Feb, added 2023) · **St Patrick's Day** (17 Mar) · **Easter Monday** · **May Bank Holiday** (1st Mon) · **June Bank Holiday** (1st Mon) · **August Bank Holiday** (1st Mon) · **October Bank Holiday** (last Mon) · **Christmas Day** (25 Dec) · **St Stephen's Day** (26 Dec). | **Public-holiday compensation — 4 options, employer's choice** | Employer picks one of: **(a)** a paid day off on the holiday; **(b)** a paid day off within a month of the holiday; **(c)** an additional day of annual leave; **(d)** an additional day's pay. | A retail worker rostered to work St Patrick's Day gets, e.g., an extra paid day off within the following month. | Employee may **request** a specific option (≥21 days' notice); employer must confirm ≥14 days before the holiday — silence defaults to a paid day off (or extra pay, if the holiday falls on the worker's normal day off). | [OWTA 1997 s.21](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/21/revised/en/html) |
| **Part-time/casual qualifying threshold** | To qualify for a holiday, an employee must have **worked ≥40 hours** for that employer in the **5 weeks** ending the day before the holiday. | A casual worker who worked 25h in the preceding 5 weeks does not qualify for that holiday. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.21(4)](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/21/revised/en/html) |
| **Weekly rest normally includes Sunday** | The §4 24h weekly-rest period is, **by default, Sunday** — deferrable only on the technical/organisational grounds in §4. | — | — | [OWTA 1997 s.13](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/13/revised/en/html) |
| **Sunday-work compensation — statutory right, "reasonable" rate** | An employee required to work Sunday is entitled to: an **allowance**, an **increased rate of pay**, **paid time off**, or a **combination** — whichever is **"reasonable having regard to all the circumstances."** No statutory %. | 🔎 WRC/Labour Court decisions have treated **time-and-a-third (+33%)** as a floor of "reasonable," with **time-and-a-half (+50%)** common practice and **double time (+100%)** seen in some retail/hospitality collective arrangements. Example: €14.15/h base × 8h Sunday hours at +33% = €150.56 total (vs €113.20 base). | Rate/form is contract/ERO-set; if a payslip doesn't show how the Sunday premium was calculated, the WRC may find the base rate itself too low and order back pay. | [OWTA 1997 s.14](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/14/revised/en/html) |

## 8. On-call & standby

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **On-site / on-call standby — counts as working time** | Standby at an employer-specified location, where the worker must be present and available to act, counts as **working time in full** under the s.2 definition — consistent with CJEU doctrine (SIMAP/Jaeger) that OWTA (as Directive-transposing legislation) is read against. | An on-site standby night at a facility counts toward the 48h weekly average and the 11h daily-rest calculation. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.2](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/2/revised/en/html); CJEU [C-303/98 *SIMAP*](https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-303/98), [C-151/02 *Jaeger*](https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-151/02) |
| **On-call from home — rest time except actual call-outs** | Where the worker chooses their own location but must be reachable, the standby period is **rest time**; only the time actually spent responding to a call-out counts as working time — mirroring the CJEU's *Matzak* reasoning on the degree of constraint imposed. | Reachable overnight from home → only the 30 minutes of an actual call-out count as working time. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.2](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/2/revised/en/html); CJEU [C-518/15 *Matzak*](https://curia.europa.eu/juris/liste.jsf?num=C-518/15) |
| **No separate statutory standby-pay regime** | OWTA doesn't set a standby/on-call allowance — any on-call fee is purely contractual (or ERO-set in covered sectors). | — | — | — none identified in current research — |

## 9. Scheduling & premium pay

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **24-hour advance notice of working hours** | Where normal daily start/finish times aren't already fixed by contract/ERO/collective agreement, the employer must notify the employee **≥24 hours before the first day of each week** of that week's start/finish times; work must then occur within the notified hours/days. | An employer publishes Monday's roster the preceding Sunday, ≥24h ahead. | Doesn't apply to seafarers/sea fishers. | [OWTA 1997 s.17](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/17/revised/en/html) |
| **Right to refuse un-notified hours** | If the 24h notice isn't given, the employee (other than Defence Forces members) has the **right to refuse** to work outside the notified reference hours/days, **without adverse consequence**. | A worker asked to cover an un-notified extra shift the same day may lawfully decline. | Exception where the need "could not reasonably have been foreseen." | [OWTA 1997 s.17](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/17/revised/en/html) |
| **Separate notice for additional/OT hours** | A **further 24h notice** is required specifically for any *additional* (discretionary/overtime) hours beyond the notified normal schedule. | Having notified Monday–Friday 9–17 for the week, the employer must give a separate ≥24h notice before adding a Saturday overtime shift. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.17](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/17/revised/en/html) |
| **Zero-hours minimum-payment protection** | If an employer requires availability but provides **less than 25%** of the contracted/expected hours in a week, the employee is owed a minimum payment: **3× the national minimum hourly rate** (or the relevant ERO rate) for the **lesser of** (i) 25% of the hours they were required to be available for, or (ii) **15 hours**. 🔎 figures per statutory paraphrase, not verbatim text. | Example: contract requires availability for 20h/week; only 2h are actually rostered. Shortfall triggers the 25%-of-20h = 5h measure (below the 15h cap) → payment = 5h × (3 × €14.15) = 5h × €42.45 = **€212.25**. | Excluded for emergency cover, short-term relief, lay-off, genuinely exceptional circumstances, or where the employee was unavailable (e.g., illness). Being merely "on call" for emergencies isn't itself "availability" under this section. | [OWTA 1997 s.18](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/18/revised/en/html) (as inserted by [Employment (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2018](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2018/act/38/revised/en/html) 🔎) |
| **Banded-hours request right** | After **12 months** on a contract that doesn't reflect actual hours worked, an employee may request (in writing) placement into one of **8 weekly-hour bands**: **A** 3–6h · **B** 6–11h · **C** 11–16h · **D** 16–21h · **E** 21–26h · **F** 26–31h · **G** 31–36h · **H** 36h+. Employer must respond within **4 weeks**. | An employee contracted for "0–10h" but averaging 24h/week over the past 12 months requests placement in Band E (21–26h). | Employer may refuse for lack of supporting evidence, significant intervening business change, an s.5 emergency, or temporary factors; doesn't apply where the banding was set via collective bargaining. | [OWTA 1997 s.18A](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/18a/revised/en/html) 🔎 (as inserted by [Employment (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 2018](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2018/act/38/revised/en/html) 🔎) |
| **No statutory reporting/show-up, split-shift, or general predictability pay** | Beyond the s.17 notice rules and the s.18 zero-hours minimum payment, Ireland has **no** separate statutory reporting-time, split-shift, or predictability-pay regime. | — | — | [OWTA 1997](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/revised/en/html) ss.17–18A — no further provision |

## 10. Leave & absence

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Annual leave — 3 calculation methods, greatest applies** | Whichever is **greatest** of: **(1)** 4 working weeks, if ≥1,365h worked in the leave year; **(2)** 1/3 working week per month in which ≥117h worked; **(3)** 8% of hours worked in the leave year, **capped at 4 working weeks**. | A worker doing 20h/week (≈1,040h/yr) uses method (3): 8% × 1,040h = 83.2h ≈ 2.4 working weeks' worth. A full-time worker easily clears 1,365h and gets the flat 4 weeks. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.19](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/19/revised/en/html) |
| **Unbroken 2-week block for ≥8-months-a-year workers** | An employee working **≥8 months** in the leave year must receive an **unbroken 2-week** period of annual leave (may include public holidays/sick days within it). | An employee who worked the full leave year must be scheduled at least one unbroken 2-week block of leave, not just single days scattered across the year. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.20](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/20/revised/en/html) |
| **Annual leave accrues during certified sick leave + 15-month carryover** | Statutory annual leave **continues to accrue** while an employee is on certified sick leave; where illness prevented taking leave within the normal year, the employee gets a **15-month** window (from the end of that leave year) to take it — beyond the ordinary carryover — and it's paid out on termination if still unused within that window. | An employee on long-term certified sick leave through the whole leave year still accrues the full 4 weeks, usable up to 15 months after that year ends. | Reforms responded to CJEU *Schultz-Hoff*/*KHS* case law. | Workplace Relations Act 2015 s.86(1) (amending [OWTA 1997 s.19](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/19/revised/en/html)) 🔎 |
| **Sick days taken during scheduled annual leave don't count as annual leave** | A day of **certified illness** falling within a scheduled annual-leave period is **not** treated as a day of annual leave — it's re-credited. | A worker on annual leave who falls ill for 2 certified days gets those 2 days back as leave, not consumed as holiday. | — | [OWTA 1997 s.19(2)](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1997/act/20/section/19/revised/en/html) |
| **Statutory sick pay (SSP)** | **5 days/year** currently (2024–2026; the original phase-in to 7 (2025)/10 (2026) days is **paused**, ⚠ not yet in force — see §1). Paid at **70% of usual daily earnings, capped at €110/day**. Requires **13 weeks'** continuous service and a certifying medical practitioner's cert. | A worker earning €200/day, certified sick, receives 70% × €200 = €140, capped down to **€110/day**. | An employer scheme that is "on the whole more beneficial" (service period, waiting time, duration, amount, reference period compared holistically) can substitute for the statutory scheme. | [Sick Leave Act 2022](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2022/act/44/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Maternity leave** | **26 weeks paid** ("ordinary" — State Maternity Benefit, ≈€299/week 2026, not an employer obligation absent a top-up) **+ up to 16 weeks additional unpaid**. **Minimum 2 weeks before + 4 weeks after** the birth are compulsory; the rest is flexible. Day-one right — **no minimum service** required. | Due 1 June → ≥2 weeks must be taken before, ≥4 weeks after; remaining weeks placed at the employee's discretion within the 26. | ~48% of employers top up State Benefit to full pay (contractual, not statutory). | [Maternity Protection Act 1994](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1994/act/34/revised/en/html) 🔎 (as amended) |
| **Paternity leave** | **2 consecutive weeks**, taken any time within the **first 26 weeks** after birth/adoption placement. Day-one right — no minimum service. | A father takes his 2 consecutive weeks in week 10 after the birth, having started the job only 3 weeks before the birth. | Covers fathers, spouses/civil partners/cohabitants of the birth mother, and same-sex partners. | [Paternity Leave and Benefit Act 2016](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2016/act/11/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Adoptive leave** | **24 consecutive weeks paid** for the adopting parent + **up to 16 weeks additional unpaid**; the non-adopting partner instead gets the standard **2 weeks' paternity leave**. | An adopting mother takes the full 24 weeks' paid adoptive leave plus a further 12 of the 16 optional unpaid weeks, while her spouse takes 2 weeks' paternity leave. | For same-sex couples, either partner may take adoptive leave while the other takes paternity leave. | Adoptive Leave Acts 1995–2005, [1995 Act](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1995/act/2/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Parent's leave** | **9 weeks per parent per child** (raised from 7 weeks, effective **1 August 2024**), paid via State Parent's Benefit (≈€299/week 2026). Must be taken within the child's first **104 weeks (2 years)** of life (or 2 years from adoption placement). | Each parent independently takes up to 9 weeks any time before the child turns 2. | Day-one right; distinct from (and in addition to) maternity/paternity/parental leave. | [Parent's Leave and Benefit Act 2019](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2019/act/35/revised/en/html) 🔎, as amended 2024 |
| **Parental leave (unpaid)** | **26 weeks unpaid per parent per child**, usable up to the child's **12th birthday** (16th for a child with a disability/long-term illness). Eligibility: **1 year's service** (pro-rated at **1 week per completed month** for 3–12 months' service). Taken as one continuous block, **two blocks of ≥6 weeks** with a ≥10-week gap, or flexibly (daily/hourly) by employer agreement. | A parent with 6 months' service takes ~6 weeks of parental leave (pro-rated). | Public holidays falling within a parental-leave block extend it by a day; annual leave coinciding with the block is rescheduled. **6 weeks'** written notice required. | [Parental Leave Act 1998](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1998/act/30/revised/en/html) (as amended) |
| **Force majeure leave (paid)** | **3 days in any 12 consecutive months**, or **5 days in any 36 consecutive months** — for **urgent family reasons** owing to the injury or illness of a listed family member, requiring the employee's **immediate presence**. Covered family: children, spouse/civil partner, *in loco parentis* relationships [standing in place of a parent], parents, grandparents, siblings, and persons in a relationship of domestic dependency residing with the employee. | A parent takes 1 day when their child is suddenly hospitalised. | Employee must confirm the leave taken as soon as reasonably practicable afterward. | [Parental Leave Act 1998 s.13](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1998/act/30/section/13/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Medical care leave (unpaid)** | Up to **5 days/year** to provide **personal care or support** to a person in need of significant care/support for a **serious medical reason**. | An employee takes 5 separate days across the year to accompany a parent undergoing chemotherapy to appointments. | Distinct from force-majeure leave (which requires *urgency*/immediate presence); this covers ongoing care needs. | [Parental Leave Act 1998](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1998/act/30/revised/en/html) (as amended by Work Life Balance and Miscellaneous Provisions Act 2023) |
| **Domestic violence leave (paid)** | **5 days paid, per rolling 12-month period**, for an employee (or a covered dependent — child, spouse, cohabitant, intimate partner) experiencing domestic violence, to access services/legal advice/relocate etc. **No service requirement.** Taken in **full-day** increments only; employer must be told "as soon as possible," with no advance-notice requirement and no right to demand details of the abuse. | An employee takes 2 non-consecutive days to attend court and relocate. | Compensation for wrongful denial capped at 20 weeks' remuneration. | [Work Life Balance and Miscellaneous Provisions Act 2023 s.7](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2023/act/8/revised/en/html) 🔎 (amending Parental Leave Act 1998) |
| **Carer's leave (unpaid, job-protected)** | **Minimum 13 weeks, maximum 104 weeks** per care recipient; requires **12 months' continuous service**. Job-protected; may be taken alongside the State's **Carer's Benefit** (a downstream social-welfare payment). | An employee takes 26 weeks' carer's leave to look after a parent needing full-time care. | — | [Carer's Leave Act 2001](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2001/act/19/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **Flexible working request (for caring purposes)** | A **right to request** (not an automatic entitlement to obtain) flexible working arrangements — reduced/flexible hours or remote work — for caring for a child (to age 12, or 16 for a disabled/long-term-ill child) or a person needing significant care. Employer may refuse with reasons. | A parent of a 10-year-old requests a 4-day week to manage school pickups; the employer must consider the request and give reasons if refusing. | Distinct from, and layered atop, the specific paid/unpaid leaves above. | [Work Life Balance and Miscellaneous Provisions Act 2023](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/2023/act/8/revised/en/html) 🔎 |
| **No general statutory bereavement leave** | Ireland has **no** stand-alone statutory bereavement-leave entitlement beyond what **force-majeure leave** (above) covers for an urgent family death/illness situation. | — | Many employers grant contractual bereavement leave (e.g., 2–3 days) — not a statutory floor. | [Parental Leave Act 1998 s.13](https://revisedacts.lawreform.ie/eli/1998/act/30/section/13/revised/en/html) 🔎 — no further provision |
