# Canada — T&A requirements

> **What this is.** The ground-truth reference for Canada's time-&-attendance legal requirements,
> grouped by topic — detailed enough to **build a day.io pay policy from**. It aims to be
> **exhaustive** and **atomic**: **one legal proposition per row**, each row self-contained (no
> "see §X" as the only content), with exact values, a worked example wherever a number is involved,
> variants, and a `Basis` that **links to the primary source** so any row can be checked against the
> law directly.
>
> **Scope: time & day-events only** — money (%, $, tax, gross-to-net) is downstream *context* here
> (kept in `Values` so a policy can be configured; the deliverable is the typed hour/day event).
> **Term convention:** every non-English (mostly Québécois French) term is glossed in English in
> brackets on first use, e.g. *Loi sur les normes du travail* [Act Respecting Labour Standards].
>
> **The one structural fact to hold onto:** Canada has **no single national T&A statute.** ~6% of
> the workforce (federally regulated industries — banks, interprovincial/international transport,
> telecom, broadcasting, pipelines, federal Crown corporations) is governed by the **Canada Labour
> Code (CLC) Part III**; the other ~94% is governed by the **Employment Standards Act (ESA)** (or
> Quebec's *Loi sur les normes du travail* [Act Respecting Labour Standards], "**LNT**"/"**ARLS**")
> of the **province where the work is physically performed** — not head office, not payroll
> location. A **collective agreement (CBA)** may sit on top of either floor (never below); Quebec
> sectoral decrees (notably **CCQ** construction) add a third, sector-wide layer. So most rows below
> cite a **provincial statute**, not the federal one — and the same rule can carry up to 13 different
> figures across the 10 provinces + 3 territories. Read the per-province `Variants`/row split as the
> operative content, not a footnote.
>
> **Legal sources & links:** Canada Labour Code Part III [CLC] on the Justice Laws consolidated-statute
> site; each province/territory's Employment Standards Act (ESA) on its own official statute portal
> (Ontario e-Laws, Québec LégisQuébec, BC Queen's Printer, etc.) or its labour-ministry guidance page
> where the statute portal isn't reliably deep-linkable; Transport Canada's Commercial Vehicle Drivers
> Hours of Service Regulations; CanLII (canlii.org) as the fallback national case-law/consolidated-
> statute search. Supplemented by the repo seed `context/worldwide-calculations/canada.md` + the prior
> `support-memos/canada.md` (parked below) + Workforce.com, Achkar Law, Samfiru Tumarkin, CFIB and
> other practitioner guides for figures not found on a primary-source page in this pass. 🔎 marks a
> figure or deep link not independently confirmed live in this pass.

## 1. Scope, classification & governing sources

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Jurisdictional routing — federal vs. province, by physical work location** | Every shift routes to (1) **CLC Part III** if the employer is in a federally regulated industry, else (2) the **ESA of the province where the work is physically performed** (not HQ, not hiring location, not payroll location; remote workers → province of residence/work). | A trucking company: intra-provincial hauls are provincial ESA; the same driver's interprovincial hauls are federal CLC — jurisdiction can shift **mid-trip**. | Remote/mobile workers crossing provinces in one pay period are governed **hour-by-hour** by each province. | [CLC Part III](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html); provincial ESAs (linked per row below) |
| **Federally regulated industry test** | Coverage is **industry-based, not size-based** — **~6%** of the workforce: banks, interprovincial/international transport (air, rail, road, marine), telecom, broadcasting, pipelines crossing provincial lines, federal Crown corporations, federal government, some First Nations band offices, longshoring, grain elevators. | A bank-branch teller = federal; a building-maintenance contractor working at that same bank branch = provincial. Intra-provincial trucking = provincial; interprovincial trucking = federal. | First Nations: band-council employees usually federal; on-reserve businesses may be provincial. | [CLC s.2 (definitions)](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-1.html) 🔎 |
| **Provincial ESA — the primary layer for ~94%** | Each of the 10 provinces + 3 territories has its **own** Employment Standards Act (or equivalent) setting OT triggers (daily and/or weekly), holiday list + pay formula, breaks, rest, vacation floor — the whole patchwork lives here. | — | 13 distinct statutory regimes (10 provinces + YT/NWT/NU); Quebec's is the *Loi sur les normes du travail* [Act Respecting Labour Standards], enforced by **CNESST** [Commission des normes, de l'équité, de la santé et de la sécurité du travail]. | [Ontario ESA 2000](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); [Quebec LNT](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/N-1.1); [BC ESA](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/96113_01) — remaining provinces linked per row below |
| **Collective agreement (CBA) — floor, never ceiling** | A CBA may **exceed** the statutory floor but **may never go below it**; any contract clause purporting to waive statutory rights is **void**, even with apparent consideration (e.g. a raise). | A CBA "all-inclusive salary covers all overtime" clause is unenforceable for non-managerial staff — OT is still owed. | Union coverage by sector: public admin ~80%, education ~75%, healthcare ~60%, utilities ~65%, construction ~35%, manufacturing ~25%, retail ~13%, finance ~10%, professional services <5%. | [Ontario ESA s.5 (no contracting out)](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎; [CLC s.168](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html) |
| **CBA / decree = its own pay-policy arrangement** | Each bargaining unit's CBA sets its **own** workweek definition, OT thresholds (may be below-statutory-hours but paid at/above statutory rate), premium schedule, banking rules, vacation tiers — model each as a **separate** policy. | An ONA [Ontario Nurses' Association] CBA sets a **37.5h** standard week with OT from the **7.5th** daily hour at 1.5×, rising to 2.0× beyond +4h. | A single hospital may run ONA (nurses) + OPSEU (clerical) + CUPE (support) + IUOE (maintenance) CBAs simultaneously — apply **per employee by classification**, not per employer. | CBA text (per bargaining unit; not independently published) |
| **Quebec CCQ construction decree — sectoral hybrid** | The ***Commission de la construction du Québec*** [Quebec Construction Commission] administers a quasi-CBA covering **all** Quebec construction workers (union or not) across 4 sectors (residential, institutional/commercial, industrial, civil-engineering/roads): daily 8h / weekly 40h thresholds, OT 1.5× (2.0× Sundays/holidays in some sectors). | A non-union residential-sector electrician is still paid per the CCQ scale and OT rules. | CCQ overrides the LNT floor where CCQ's is higher; modelled as a policy layered over the LNT. | [CCQ — décret/conventions](https://www.ccq.org/en) 🔎 |
| **Quebec Decree sectors (non-construction)** | Certain other Quebec sectors (e.g. security guards) have their own government decree, applying sector-wide even to non-union employers. | Security-guard sector: **44h/week** OT threshold (vs. the general 40h). | Sector-specific decrees layered over the LNT. | [Quebec decree regulations — LégisQuébec](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/rc) 🔎 |
| **Managerial / professional OT exemption — duties test, no salary-alone rule** | Managers and certain professionals are exempt from OT (and often from hours-of-work rules) in most ESAs, determined by a **duties test** — unlike the US, **salary level alone never exempts**; a non-managerial salaried worker still earns OT. | A salaried non-managerial admin assistant earning $70k/yr still accrues OT past the weekly/daily threshold. | Assign clearly-exempt managers a no-OT policy; borderline duties-test cases need manual classification. | [Ontario ESA — exemptions O.Reg 285/01](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/regulation/010285) 🔎 |
| **Independent-contractor misclassification** | A worker found to be misclassified is entitled to **all** ESA/CLC rights **retroactively** (OT, vacation, holiday pay, leave). | A "contractor" controlled like an employee (fixed hours, tools provided, exclusivity) is reclassified and back-paid. | Worker-classification flag at onboarding should drive whether ESA/CLC calculations even apply. | [CLC Part III](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html); [Ontario ESA s.5](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |
| **Probationary employees — full ESA/CLC from day 1** | Probationary status does **not** reduce OT, vacation-pay accrual, or statutory-holiday entitlement; the **only** difference is a reduced/eliminated termination-notice period. | A new hire's first week already accrues vacation pay and OT normally. | — | [Ontario ESA s.54 (notice of termination)](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎; [CLC s.230](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-11.html) 🔎 |
| **Minimum working age — federally regulated work: 18** | Federal (CLC-regulated) employers may not employ anyone under **18**, raised from 17 effective **12 Jun 2023**. | An 18th-birthday hire date is the earliest a federally regulated employer can roster a worker, regardless of the province. | Provincial general minimum ages are set independently — see the following rows. | [CLC Minimum Age Regulations](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/regulations/SOR-2023-160/index.html) 🔎 |
| **Minimum working age — Alberta: 13 (light work; most jobs from 15)** | A 13-year-old may do "light work" (e.g. newspaper delivery, light retail clerking); most other jobs require age **15**; hazardous work requires **18**. | A 13-year-old in Alberta may deliver newspapers or do light retail clerking. | Type-of-work and hours restrictions layer on top (see §4/§5). | [Alberta Employment Standards — young workers](https://www.alberta.ca/employment-standards-rules) 🔎 |
| **Minimum working age — Ontario: 14 (no work during school hours)** | General floor **14**; no work during school hours; some jobs (e.g. farm work) permit younger ages under specific conditions. | A 14-year-old in Ontario may work after school but not during school hours. | — | [Ontario ESA — employment of children](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |
| **Minimum working age — BC: 16 general (12–14 with consent + registration)** | General floor **16**; ages **12–14** permitted only with written parental consent **and** employer registration with the Director of Employment Standards. | A 13-year-old in BC may be hired only if the employer has registered the light-work role and holds parental consent. | — | [BC ESA — hiring children (Part 2)](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/96113_01) |
| **Minimum working age — Quebec: under-14 generally barred; under-16 school-hours/evening limits** | Under-**14**: generally barred from employment (narrow exceptions, e.g. family business, some artistic work); under-**16**: barred from work during school hours and subject to evening-hour limits. | A 15-year-old in Quebec cannot be scheduled during school hours regardless of parental consent. | — | [Quebec LNT — minors](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/N-1.1) 🔎 |
| **Minimum working age — Manitoba: 13–15 requires a Young Worker Readiness Certificate first** | Workers aged **13–15** must first complete a government-run Young Worker Readiness Certificate Course before starting employment. | A 14-year-old must show proof of course completion before a Manitoba employer can roster their first shift. | — | [Manitoba Employment Standards — young workers](https://www.gov.mb.ca/labour/standards/) 🔎 |
| **Commercial-vehicle Hours-of-Service (HOS) overlay** | Federal Commercial Vehicle Drivers HOS Regulations apply **on top of** ESA/CLC for commercial drivers and cap the shift **before** OT calculations apply (see §4 for the full HOS table). | A driver capped at 13h daily driving under HOS is scheduled within that limit regardless of the province's OT trigger. | Trucking-specific; enforced at scheduling time, not just payroll. | [Commercial Vehicle Drivers Hours of Service Regulations, SOR/2005-313](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/regulations/SOR-2005-313/index.html) |
| **Right to disconnect — Ontario policy mandate (≥25 employees)** | Employers with **≥25 employees** must have a written right-to-disconnect policy, in force since 2022. | An Ontario retailer with 30 staff must maintain (not necessarily disclose specifics of) a written R2D policy. | Affects T&A by shaping whether after-hours emails/calls count as work time (see §2) and on-call classification (see §8). | [Ontario ESA — Working for Workers Act, 2021 (R2D reg)](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |
| **Right to disconnect — federal policy mandate (pending)** | CLC amendments require all federally regulated private-sector employers (and Crown corps) to develop a right-to-disconnect policy, in consultation with employees (≥90 days' comment period). ⚠ Being brought into force; regulations expected to require a general rule on after-hours work-related communication. | — | Quebec has no specific R2D statute yet, though *de facto* expected under Bill 96 context. | [CLC amendments — canada.ca guidance](https://www.canada.ca/en/employment-social-development/services/labour-standards.html) 🔎 |
| **⚠ Ontario Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act (gig workers)** | In force **1 Jul 2025**; extends baseline rights to platform workers (e.g. rideshare/delivery) regardless of contractor status: **minimum wage for time spent on an assigned work assignment**, regular pay periods, written pay-calculation information, written notice before removing platform access. | A delivery-app worker must be paid minimum wage for the duration of an accepted, in-progress assignment. | Ontario-only; a **new worker-classification regime**, not a modification of standard-employee OT/rest rules. | [Digital Platform Workers' Rights Act, 2022 (ON) — ontario.ca laws register](https://www.ontario.ca/laws) 🔎 |

## 2. Working-time definition

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Always counted as work time** | Time at the workstation ready to work (even if no work assigned); mandatory training (incl. off-site); required travel **between worksites** during the workday; required meetings even off-shift; time donning/removing **required PPE** at the worksite (ON, BC — case law); authorized paid rest/coffee breaks where required. | A worker required to don hazmat gear on-site before clocking a shift start has that time counted as work time in ON/BC. | Federal: rest breaks are paid by statute. Quebec: coffee breaks count as work if the employee must remain available. | [Ontario ESA — hours of work](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); ON/BC case law via [CanLII](https://www.canlii.org/en/) 🔎 |
| **Not counted as work time** | Commute from home to first worksite and from last worksite to home; unpaid meal breaks where the employee is **free to leave** the premises; at-home on-call with reasonable freedom (see §8); voluntary attendance at optional events. | A worker who can leave the building during an unpaid 30-min lunch is not "working" during it. | — | [Ontario ESA — hours of work](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) |
| **On-call / standby classification** | On-premise standby always counts; at-home reachability with reasonable freedom does not; tightly restricted at-home reachability is fact-specific (generally counted). The full classification matrix, with worked examples for each restriction level, is at §8. | A worker confined on-site overnight is "working" the whole period; the fact-specific at-home cases are worked through in §8. | — | [Ontario ESA — hours of work](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); case law via [CanLII](https://www.canlii.org/en/) 🔎 |
| **Travel time** | Travel **during** the workday between sites is paid at the regular rate. Travel **from home to a remote/temporary site beyond the regular commute** is typically paid in **Ontario** (case law), **Quebec** (statutory) and **federally** (statutory, if it exceeds the usual commute). | A worker sent to a client site 90 minutes away (vs. a normal 20-min commute) is paid for the extra travel time in these jurisdictions. | Travel time generally **does not count toward the OT trigger** in some provinces — check the CBA. | ON case law via [CanLII](https://www.canlii.org/en/) 🔎; [Quebec LNT](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/N-1.1) 🔎; [CLC Part III](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html) |
| **Training time** | Mandatory training = paid work time, **counts toward OT**. Voluntary, employer-encouraged off-shift training is generally not work time but is ambiguous case-by-case. | A required 2-hour compliance webinar after hours is paid and counts toward that week's OT total. | — | [Ontario ESA — hours of work](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); case law via [CanLII](https://www.canlii.org/en/) 🔎 |
| **Time-clock rounding / tolerance** | **No federal or provincial regulation** on rounding (unlike the US FLSA 7-minute rule). Rounding must be **neutral over time** (cannot systematically favour the employer). Best practice: round to the nearest minute with a configurable grace period (e.g. 5 min) at shift start/end. | — | CBAs frequently **prohibit rounding entirely** or cap it to 1-minute precision. | (no statutory provision); CBA practice |
| **Cross-midnight shift / workday definition** | A shift crossing midnight (e.g. 23:00–07:00) defines the "workday" as the **24-hour period starting at the shift's start time**, not calendar midnight — avoids double-counting hours across two calendar days for daily-OT purposes. | A worker on 23:00–07:00 has all 8 hours attributed to the workday that began at 23:00, not split 1h/7h across two calendar days. | Matters most in the daily-OT provinces (BC/AB/MB/SK). | ESA interpretation practice — no single citable article 🔎 |
| **Right-to-disconnect classification of after-hours contact** | Where a right-to-disconnect policy exists (ON ≥25 employees; federal, pending), it shapes — but does not by itself convert — whether after-hours emails/calls count as work time; classification still follows the ordinary work-time test. | A late-night email requiring no substantive response is not automatically "work time" merely because an R2D policy exists. | Quebec has no R2D statute yet. | [Ontario ESA — R2D reg](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎; [CLC amendments](https://www.canada.ca/en/employment-social-development/services/labour-standards.html) 🔎 |

## 3. Overtime

### 3a. Onset / trigger

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Federal — weekly >40h** | OT after **40h/week**; each week stands alone (no cross-week averaging absent a registered agreement). | A federal worker doing 46h in a week has 6 OT hours. | No daily trigger for general federal workers (only regulated drivers, via HOS-adjacent rules). | [CLC s.171](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html) |
| **Quebec — weekly >40h** | OT after **40h/week** (LNT). | A Quebec worker doing 46h in a week has 6 OT hours. | No daily trigger. | [Quebec LNT s.52](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/N-1.1) 🔎 |
| **Manitoba — weekly >40h** | OT after **40h/week**. | A Manitoba worker doing 46h has 6 OT hours. | No daily trigger. | [Manitoba Employment Standards Code](https://www.gov.mb.ca/labour/standards/) 🔎 |
| **Newfoundland & Labrador — weekly >40h** | OT after **40h/week**. | An NL worker doing 46h has 6 OT hours. | No daily trigger. | [NL Labour Standards Act](https://www.gov.nl.ca/dgsnl/employment/labour-standards/) 🔎 |
| **Ontario — weekly >44h** | OT after **44h/week**. | An Ontario worker doing 50h has 6 OT hours (44 reg + 6 OT). | No daily trigger. | [Ontario ESA s.22](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) |
| **Alberta — weekly >44h** | OT after **44h/week**. | An Alberta worker doing 50h has 6 OT hours. | Alberta additionally has an 8h **daily** trigger — see the next row and §3e (greater-of rule). | [Alberta Employment Standards Code](https://www.alberta.ca/employment-standards-rules) 🔎 |
| **New Brunswick — weekly >44h** | OT after **44h/week**. | A New Brunswick worker doing 50h has 6 OT hours. | No daily trigger. | [New Brunswick Employment Standards Act](https://www2.gnb.ca/content/gnb/en/departments/petl-epft/People.html) 🔎 |
| **Nova Scotia — weekly >48h** | OT after **48h/week**. | A Nova Scotia worker doing 52h has 4 OT hours. | NS's OT rate is calculated on **minimum wage**, not the actual rate (see §3b). No daily trigger. | [NS Labour Standards Code](https://www.novascotia.ca/lae/employmentrights/) 🔎 |
| **PEI — weekly >48h** | OT after **48h/week**. | A PEI worker doing 52h has 4 OT hours. | No daily trigger. | [PEI Employment Standards Act](https://www.princeedwardisland.ca/en/topic/employment-standards) 🔎 |
| **BC — daily >8h** | OT after **8h in a single day**, independent of the weekly total. | A BC worker doing a single 10h day has 2 OT hours that day even in an otherwise light week. | Stacks with BC's own weekly-40h check (§3e "first-8h-only" rule) and BC's 12h double-time tier (§3b). | [BC ESA Part 4](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/96113_01) |
| **Alberta — daily >8h** | OT after **8h in a single day**, independent of the weekly total. | An Alberta worker doing a single 10h day has 2 OT hours that day. | Interacts with the 44h weekly trigger via the "greater of" rule (§3e). | [Alberta Employment Standards Code](https://www.alberta.ca/employment-standards-rules) 🔎 |
| **Manitoba — daily >8h** | OT after **8h in a single day**. | A Manitoba worker doing a single 10h day has 2 OT hours that day. | — | [Manitoba Employment Standards Code](https://www.gov.mb.ca/labour/standards/) 🔎 |
| **Saskatchewan — daily >8h (or >10h on an approved 4-day week)** | OT after **8h**, or **10h** if the employee is on an approved 4-day compressed week. | A Saskatchewan worker on a standard week gets OT past 8h/day; one on an approved 4×10 week gets OT only past 10h/day. | — | [Saskatchewan Employment Act](https://www.saskatchewan.ca/business/employment-standards) 🔎 |
| **Yukon / NWT / Nunavut — daily >8h** | OT after **8h in a single day**, matching the western-provinces pattern. | A Yukon worker doing a 10h day has 2 OT hours. | Territorial ESAs largely mirror BC/AB structure. | Territorial ESAs (Yukon, NWT, Nunavut employment standards) 🔎 |
| **Regular rate for salaried employees** | `Regular hourly rate = Weekly salary ÷ standard weekly hours.` If the contract is silent on standard hours: most provinces assume **40** (or actual usual hours); **Ontario assumes 44** (the OT threshold); **BC assumes 40**. | A $2,200/week Ontario salary with a silent contract → $50/h regular rate (2,200 ÷ 44). | — | [Ontario ESA s.22](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); [BC ESA Part 4](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/96113_01) |
| **Salary does not exempt from OT** | Unlike the US, salary status alone **never** exempts a non-managerial employee from OT; "salary covers all hours" clauses are **unenforceable**. | A salaried, non-managerial analyst working 50h/week (44h ON threshold) is owed OT on the extra 6h regardless of the salary agreement. | Managerial/professional duties-test exemption is the only carve-out (§1). | [Ontario ESA s.5 (no contracting out)](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |
| **Multiple pay rates in one workweek** | Default (most provinces): a **weighted average** of the rates is used for OT. Alternative (ON, BC): pay OT at the **rate applicable to the hour that triggered OT**. `Weighted rate = (Hours₁×Rate₁ + Hours₂×Rate₂) ÷ Total hours.` | A worker doing 30h at $20/h and 20h at $30/h (50h total, ON 44h threshold): weighted rate = (30×20+20×30)/50 = $24/h; OT = 6h × $24 × 1.5. | CBAs frequently override with the **higher** of the two rates. | [Ontario ESA — overtime guide](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |

### 3b. Rate & bands

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Standard OT multiplier — 1.5× nationwide floor** | **1.5×** the regular rate is the statutory floor for OT everywhere in Canada. | $25/h regular rate → $37.50/h OT rate. | CBAs commonly add a **2.0×** tier after further hours (see next row + §3e). | [CLC s.174](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/L-2/page-9.html); [Ontario ESA s.22](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41); [Quebec LNT s.55](https://www.legisquebec.gouv.qc.ca/en/document/cs/N-1.1) 🔎 |
| **BC double-time — >12h/day** | BC alone stacks a second tier: **1.5×** for hours 8–12 in a day, **2.0×** beyond **12h/day**. | A 14h BC day: 8 reg + 4h @1.5× + 2h @2.0×. | Some CBAs elsewhere add a similar 2.0× tier after a set number of weekly/daily OT hours. | [BC ESA Part 4](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/96113_01) |
| **NS — OT calculated on minimum wage, not actual rate** | The 1.5× OT premium in Nova Scotia is computed on the **provincial minimum wage**, not the worker's actual hourly rate (a statutory floor calculation quirk); the base rate is still paid for all hours worked. 🔎 confirm current administrative practice | A $22/h NS worker's OT premium portion is calculated off minimum wage, not $22 — the base $22 is still paid for all hours; only the *extra* 0.5× uses minimum wage as the reference. | New Brunswick applies a related minimum-wage-referenced calculation. | [NS Labour Standards Code](https://www.novascotia.ca/lae/employmentrights/) 🔎 |
| **NB — OT calculated on minimum wage, not actual rate** | Same minimum-wage-referenced OT-premium quirk as Nova Scotia. 🔎 confirm current administrative practice | — | Alberta similarly ties reporting-pay minimums to minimum wage (see §9). | [NB Employment Standards Act](https://www2.gnb.ca/content/gnb/en/departments/petl-epft/People.html) 🔎 |
| **Newfoundland & Labrador — complex OT formula** | NL's weekly-OT rate is **1.5× minimum wage + a premium component**; distinct from the simple 1.5×-actual-rate model used elsewhere. 🔎 exact formula unconfirmed in current research | — | — | [NL Labour Standards Act](https://www.gov.nl.ca/dgsnl/employment/labour-standards/) 🔎 |
| **Federal motor-vehicle-operator OT (city / highway drivers)** | **"City" operator:** OT beyond **9h/day or 45h/week**. **"Highway" operator:** OT beyond **60h/week**. | A federally regulated city courier driver working 10h in a day has 1 OT hour even though the general federal daily trigger doesn't otherwise exist. | Sector-specific carve-out inside CLC's motor-transport regulations; distinct from the general 40h/week federal rule. | [Motor Vehicle Operators Hours of Work Regulations, C.R.C. c.990](https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/regulations/) 🔎 |
| **Commission / piecework regular rate + half-time premium** | `Regular rate = Total wages earned in period ÷ Total hours worked.` OT pays the **half-time** premium only (0.5×) on OT hours, since the regular rate already includes the base pay for those hours. | Ontario commission worker: $1,800 earned over 50h → regular rate $36/h; OT hours = 50−44 = 6; OT premium = 6 × $36 × 0.5 = $108; total = $1,908. | — | [Ontario ESA s.22](https://www.ontario.ca/laws/statute/00e41) 🔎 |

### 3c. Caps & counters

## Governing sources — who actually sets the OT numbers

The compliance answer is **conditional on jurisdiction**, decided per shift by where the work is *physically
performed* (not HQ, not payroll location). So "do we support Canada?" really means "**which regime applies —
federal, which province, and is a CBA/decree layered on top?**"

| Layer | Instrument | Sets the operative T&A numbers? |
|---|---|---|
| Federal statute | **Canada Labour Code Part III (CLC)** | **Yes — for the ~6% in federally regulated industries** (banks, interprovincial/international transport, telecom, broadcasting, pipelines, federal Crown corps). Weekly OT >40h · 8h daily rest · 32h OT threshold in stat-holiday weeks. Coverage is **industry-based, not size-based** |
| Provincial/territorial statute | **Employment Standards Act (ESA)** of the province of work | **Yes — the PRIMARY layer for the other ~94%.** Each ESA sets its own OT triggers (daily and/or weekly), holiday list + pay formula, breaks, rest, vacation floor — this is where the patchwork lives |
| Collective agreement (CBA) | Union contract per bargaining unit | **On top of the statutory floor** — may exceed, **never below** (`s.` contracting-out is void). Heavily prevalent (public admin ~80%, education ~75%, healthcare ~60%); each CBA is modelled as its **own pay policy** |
| Quebec sectoral | **CCQ construction decree** / Quebec Decree sectors | **Yes, sector-wide** — decree/sectoral rates apply even to **non-union** employers in that sector; modelled as a policy layered over ARLS |

**Jurisdictional dimension to carry throughout:** every rule below can be province-conditional. Daily-OT
provinces map onto **S4** (per-day surplus); weekly-OT provinces onto **S5** (weekly trigger); the
**averaging agreements** and **stat-holiday-pay averaging** are the `[ABS]` gaps.
