# United States — T&A requirements

> **What this is.** The ground-truth reference for the United States' time-&-attendance legal
> requirements, detailed enough to **build a day.io pay policy from**. It aims to be **exhaustive**
> and **atomic**: **one legal proposition per row**, each row self-contained (no "see §X" as the
> only content), with exact values, a worked example wherever a number is involved, variants, and a
> `Basis` that **links to the primary source** so any row can be checked against the law directly.
> Exhaustive at the **federal (FLSA) floor**, sampled against **California, New York, and Texas** as
> the three state archetypes (deep daily-OT + penalty hours; federal-mirroring + distinctive daily
> premium; pure federal baseline).
>
> **Scope: time & day-events only** — money (%, $, tax, gross-to-net, the "regular rate" dollar
> figure) is downstream *context* here (kept in `Values` so a policy can be configured; the
> deliverable is the typed hour/day event and its quantity — e.g. `OT_WEEKLY: 3h`, not "$45 of
> overtime pay").
> **Term convention:** every non-obvious US legal/labor term is glossed in English in brackets on
> first use (most US terms are already English, so this is lighter than in non-English-speaking
> jurisdictions — but jargon like *FLSA*, *EAP exemption*, *8/80*, *§7(k)*, *FWW* is glossed).
>
> **The one structural fact that shapes this whole document.** The US stacks rules in a fixed order
> of **increasing specificity**, applying whichever layer is **most protective of the employee** at
> each point of conflict: **(1) the FLSA** [Fair Labor Standards Act] federal floor, which is
> **deliberately thin** — one premium (weekly-40 at 1.5×), no daily OT, no meal/rest-break mandate,
> no holiday pay, no night premium → **(2) state law** (may add, never subtract — California is the
> deepest daily-OT/penalty-hour state; Texas adds essentially nothing) → **(3) industry/occupation
> overlays** (hospital 8/80, public-safety §7(k), motor-carrier, agriculture, retail commission
> §7(i)) → **(4) local ordinances** (NYC Fair Workweek and similar city predictive-scheduling laws)
> → **(5) CBA** [collective-bargaining agreement] **/ employer policy** (may only improve on the
> statutory floor). The wage-and-hour law that governs is the law of the **state where the work is
> physically performed** — never the employee's residence or the employer's HQ. So almost every rich
> operative number in this document (daily OT, penalty hours, spread-of-hours, predictability pay)
> lives at layer (2)–(4), not the federal floor.
>
> **Legal sources & links:** primary citations link to the **Cornell Legal Information Institute**
> [LII] mirror of the U.S. Code and Code of Federal Regulations (`law.cornell.edu` — the federal
> register `ecfr.gov` blocks automated fetches, so LII is used as the stable federal mirror, the way
> AustLII mirrors the FWA for Australia); **California** statute links go to
> `leginfo.legislature.ca.gov`, Wage Orders to `dir.ca.gov`; **New York** statute links go to
> `nysenate.gov`, NYCRR regulations to `dol.ny.gov` (deep-link 🔎, DOL doesn't expose stable
> per-section URLs); **Texas** links go to `statutes.capitol.texas.gov`; case law links to
> `courtlistener.com` search (🔎 — free case databases don't expose stable per-opinion URLs without a
> docket lookup). Seed: `context/worldwide-calculations/united-states.md` (2026-07, FLSA + CA/NY/TX)
> + the prior `support-memos/united-states.md` (preserved verbatim in the parked appendix) + web
> research on FMLA, state paid-sick/paid-family-leave, bereavement/jury/voting leave, and FLSA
> recordkeeping. 🔎 marks a figure or deep link to confirm (primary sources disagreed, or a
> government page could not be fetched directly and a secondary source or the register's base page
> was used instead).

## 1. Scope, classification & governing sources

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Coverage precedes calculation — exempt vs non-exempt** | Only **non-exempt** employees are entitled to overtime and need an OT calculation at all. **Exempt** (EAP [Executive/Administrative/Professional] and similar) employees get **none** — the whole OT pipeline is skipped for them. | A salaried marketing manager who passes all 3 EAP tests gets no OT calc even at 55h/week; a salaried admin assistant who fails the duties test needs the full non-exempt pipeline. | The single most consequential US classification; per-employee, per-jurisdiction, date-effective. | [FLSA 29 U.S.C. §213(a)(1)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/213); [29 CFR Part 541](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-541) |
| **The 3 EAP exemption tests — all 3 must pass** | **(1) Salary-basis** — paid a fixed salary not reduced for quality/quantity of work. **(2) Salary-level** — salary ≥ the applicable **$/week threshold** (below). Up to **10%** of the federal threshold may be nondiscretionary bonus/commission paid ≥annually. **(3) Duties** — primary duties genuinely match Executive/Administrative/Professional work; **job title is irrelevant**. | An employee titled "Manager" who spends 90% of time on the same manual tasks as their reports fails the duties test → non-exempt regardless of salary or title. | Outside sales, doctors/lawyers/teachers, and computer employees skip the salary tests entirely (below). | [29 CFR §541.100 ff.](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/541.100) |
| **Federal EAP salary thresholds (2026)** | Standard EAP: **$684/week ($35,568/yr)**. HCE [Highly Compensated Employee]: **$107,432/yr** total comp (incl. ≥$684/wk salary/fee basis, relaxed duties test). Computer-employee alternative: **$684/week OR $27.63/hour**. | A computer programmer paid $30/hr and doing qualifying duties can be exempt on the hourly alternative alone — no salary-basis test applies to that path. | Outside sales / bona-fide doctors, lawyers, teachers: **no salary test at all**. | [29 CFR §541.600 ff.](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/541.600) |
| **⚠ Threshold currency caution — don't hard-code** | The DOL's 2024 rule (would have raised EAP to $844/wk Jul 2024, $1,128/wk Jan 2025; HCE to $132,964) was **vacated nationwide** (*State of Texas v. DOL*, E.D. Tex., 15 Nov 2024). DOL dropped its appeal and on **14 May 2026** re-codified the **2019 thresholds** ($684/wk; $107,432 HCE) as the regulatory text. | A worker paid $900/week who was "exempt" under the vacated 2024 rule reverted to needing the duties test scrutinized against the $684/wk floor once the rule was struck. | Treat every salary threshold as **date-effective config** — this number has moved and reverted twice in three years. | 🔎 [*State of Texas v. DOL*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=State+of+Texas+v.+Department+of+Labor+overtime) (E.D. Tex. 2024); [29 CFR Part 541](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-541) (14 May 2026 technical amendment) |
| **Blue-collar & first-responders can NEVER be EAP-exempt** | Manual/physical-skill workers (construction, production, maintenance, operators, laborers) and first responders (police, fire, paramedics, EMTs, correctional officers, park rangers) are **non-exempt regardless of pay or rank** — the EAP exemptions structurally cannot reach them. | A highly-paid union electrician salaried at $150k/yr is still non-exempt and owed OT past 40h/week. | First responders typically fall under §7(k) work periods instead (§3c). | [29 CFR §541.3](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/541.3) |
| **California-raised EAP salary threshold** | EAP exemption salary floor: **$70,304/yr ($1,352/wk)** (= 2× state MW × 2080h); computer-professional exemption **$58.85/hr ($122,573.13/yr)**; licensed-physician exemption **$107.17/hr**. | A $60,000/yr "exempt" California manager is **non-exempt** under this state threshold even though $60k clears the federal $35,568 floor. | Resolved by the employee's **work-location** state, not residence or company HQ. | 🔎 [CA Labor Code §515](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=515) |
| **New York-raised EAP salary threshold** | EAP exemption salary floor: **$66,300/yr ($1,275.00/wk)** NYC/Long Island/Westchester; **$62,353.20/yr ($1,199.10/wk)** rest of state. **No** salary threshold on the professional exemption (federal $684/wk floor applies). | A $60,000/yr "exempt" manager in NYC is **non-exempt** under the NY threshold even though $60k clears the federal $35,568 floor. | Resolved by the employee's **work-location** state, not residence or company HQ. | 🔎 [NY 12 NYCRR §142-2.14](https://dol.ny.gov/labor-laws-and-regulations) |
| **Texas — no state-raised EAP salary threshold** | Texas imposes no state EAP salary floor; the federal **$684/wk** threshold applies unchanged. | A $35,568/yr Texas manager who clears the federal floor and passes the duties test is exempt — no separate state figure to check. | — | [29 CFR §541.600](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/541.600) (federal by reference) |
| **Reclassification is a retroactive trigger** | A worker reclassified **exempt → non-exempt** with a past effective date requires **every workweek since that date** to be recomputed for OT. | A misclassification correction dated back 6 months reopens 26 workweeks of OT calculation. | Also triggered by a retroactive non-discretionary bonus or a punch correction after pay close (§3d). | FLSA general enforcement; [29 CFR Part 778](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-778) |
| **FLSA coverage — enterprise or individual** | **Enterprise coverage:** business has **≥$500,000** annual volume, or is a hospital/school/government agency (covers essentially all employees). **Individual coverage:** the specific employee engages in interstate commerce or production of goods for commerce (broad in practice). | A small local retailer under $500k revenue may still owe FLSA overtime to an employee who regularly processes out-of-state card payments (individual coverage). | Coverage is largely an employer-level flag; the interesting per-worker gate is exempt/non-exempt (above). | [29 U.S.C. §203(r)–(s)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/203) |
| **Special-population overlays — own threshold/work-period/coverage** | Hospital/residential-care staff (8/80 work period), police/fire (§7(k) 7–28-day period), motor-carrier drivers (OT-exempt, small-vehicle exception), agricultural employees (OT-exempt), retail/service commissioned staff (§7(i) OT-exempt if conditions hold), seasonal amusement staff (exempt from MW+OT), live-in domestic workers (OT-exempt, MW still owed), government-contract laborers (prevailing wage), tipped employees (tip-credit + dual-jobs tracking), minors (hour/time-of-day limits). | A hospital elects the 8/80 period for its nursing staff instead of the standard 40h/week workweek (detail in §3). | Each needs a per-worker flag and its own overlay ruleset — detailed in the relevant section below. | [29 U.S.C. §213](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/213) (various subsections); [29 CFR Part 553](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-553) (+ Parts [552](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-552), [778](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-778), [779](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-779), [782](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-782)) |
| **Multi-state work — law of the state where work is physically performed** | The applicable wage-and-hour law is the state where the work happens, **not** residence or HQ. Safest default: **per-minute state attribution**; weekly-40 OT applies across **all** hours combined regardless of state. | A worker doing 3 days in CA + 2 in NV in one workweek: the CA days follow CA daily-OT rules, the NV days follow NV rules, and federal weekly-40 OT applies to the combined total. | Alternatives: predominant-state rule, strictest-state rule, home-state-with-exceptions — per-minute is the safest default. | Choice-of-law doctrine (common law); [FLSA weekly OT](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/207) applies nationwide |
| **Joint employment — hours aggregated across employers** | When two employers jointly employ a worker (staffing agency + client; parent + subsidiary), hours worked for **both** are aggregated for FLSA overtime purposes. | An agency temp who works 25h for the agency's Client A and 20h for Client B in one workweek is owed OT on the combined 45h if both are joint employers. | Fact-intensive joint-employer test (control, supervision, integration). | [29 CFR Part 791](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-791) |
| **Minors (under 18) — federal age tiers** | **Under 14:** generally barred from employment (narrow entertainment/agricultural exceptions). **14–15:** max **3h/school day, 8h/non-school day, 18h/school week, 40h/non-school week**; hours only **7:00–19:00** (to 21:00, 1 June–Labor Day). **16–17:** federally **unrestricted hours**, but barred from named hazardous occupations. | A 15-year-old cannot be scheduled past 19:00 on a school night even in summer unless it's between 1 June and Labor Day. | States may add stricter limits — apply the **more protective** rule (Texas variant, §5). | [FLSA Child Labor provisions, 29 CFR Part 570](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-570) |
| **Tipped employees — dual-jobs classification** | Tipped workers doing both tip-producing work (serving) and tip-supporting side work (prep, rolling silverware) need per-minute/per-task classification in stricter states; the federal "80/20" rule has been in litigation (2024 rule **vacated** by the 5th Cir., *Restaurant Law Center v. DOL*). | A server who spends >20% of a shift on side work in a jurisdiction still applying an 80/20-style test loses the tip credit for that excess time. | NY hospitality applies its own stricter tip-credit tracking (§9, §12 in the seed). | [29 U.S.C. §203(m)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/203); 🔎 [*Restaurant Law Center v. DOL*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=Restaurant+Law+Center+v.+Department+of+Labor) (5th Cir.) |

## 2. Working-time definition

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **"Suffered or permitted" doctrine** | If the employer **knows or has reason to know** work is happening, that time is compensable — **even if unauthorized** or against a no-OT policy. Discipline is separate from pay; the time must still be recorded and paid. | An employee who stays 30 extra minutes without instruction, observed by a manager, must be paid for that time even though it wasn't ordered. | — | [29 CFR §785.11–.13](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.11) |
| **Continuous workday doctrine** | Once the first **principal activity** begins, all time until the last principal activity is generally compensable, except bona fide meal periods and other specifically excluded breaks. | Donning required PPE at the start of shift starts the compensable workday even before the "official" clock-in. | — | 🔎 [*IBP, Inc. v. Alvarez*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=IBP+v.+Alvarez+2005) (2005) |
| **Short rest break — ≤20 min, compensable** | Any break of **≤20 minutes** counts as hours worked and toward the OT threshold. | A 15-minute coffee break is paid and OT-counting. | — | [29 CFR §785.18](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.18) |
| **Bona fide meal break — ≥30 min, non-compensable** | A meal break of **≥30 minutes**, with the employee **completely relieved of duty**, is unpaid and does **not** count toward OT. A meal interrupted by duty is fully compensable. | A 30-minute lunch where the employee is asked to keep answering the phone is a compensable, OT-counting "meal break interrupted by duty." | State law can be stricter (CA/NY mandate the break itself — §4). | [29 CFR §785.19](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.19) |
| **Waiting time — "engaged to wait" vs "waiting to be engaged"** | **Engaged to wait** (required location, can't use time freely) = compensable. **Waiting to be engaged** (free to use time for personal purposes) = not compensable. | A driver stuck at a loading dock unable to leave is "engaged to wait" (paid); the same driver off-duty at a hotel between legs of a haul is "waiting to be engaged" (unpaid). | The distinction also governs on-call classification (§8). | [29 CFR §785.14–.17](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.14) |
| **Travel-time decision tree** | Home-to-work commute = **not compensable**. Inter-site travel during the workday = **compensable**. Special one-day out-of-town assignment = compensable (minus normal commute, employer's option). Overnight travel during normal working hours (any day) = compensable; outside normal hours as a passenger = not compensable. | An employee sent to a same-day meeting 2 hours away: the drive is compensable (minus their normal commute time); the same trip as an overnight passenger outside work hours is not. | — | [29 CFR Part 785, Subpart E](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/part-785/subpart-E); [Portal-to-Portal Act, 29 U.S.C. §251 ff.](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/251) |
| **Donning/doffing — Portal-to-Portal carve-out** | Ordinary commuting and preliminary/postliminary activities are **excluded**, but anything **"integral and indispensable"** to the principal activity (mandatory PPE, knife-sharpening for meat-cutters) is **compensable** and starts the continuous workday. | A meatpacking worker required to don cut-resistant gear on-site: that time is paid, and the workday clock starts there. | — | [Portal-to-Portal Act, 29 U.S.C. §251 ff.](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/251) (1947); 🔎 [*IBP v. Alvarez*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=IBP+v.+Alvarez+2005) (2005) |
| **Training-time — four-factor test** | Training is **non-compensable only if all four** hold: **(1)** outside regular hours, **(2)** voluntary, **(3)** not directly job-related, **(4)** no productive work performed. Fail any one → compensable. | A mandatory after-hours safety course fails factor (2) → fully paid. | — | [29 CFR §785.27–.32](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.27) |
| **Sleep time — ≥24h shifts only** | On shifts **≥24 hours**, up to **8 hours** of sleep time may be excluded by agreement if adequate facilities exist and sleep is usually uninterrupted; interruptions for work are paid; if interruptions cut sleep below **5 hours**, the **entire** period becomes compensable. On shifts **<24 hours**, sleep time is **always compensable** — no exclusion. | A 24-hour firefighter shift can exclude 8 sleep hours by agreement; a 16-hour shift cannot exclude any sleep time. | — | [29 CFR §785.22](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/785.22) |
| **PUMP Act — lactation breaks** | **Reasonable break time** to express milk for **1 year** after birth, each time needed, plus a **private space that is not a bathroom**. Unpaid **only if fully relieved of duty**; paid if concurrent with an otherwise-paid break. | A nursing employee pumping during her normal paid 15-minute break stays paid for it; an additional unpaid 20-minute pump break with no work performed is unpaid. | — | [PUMP for Nursing Mothers Act, 29 U.S.C. §218d](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/218d) (2022–2023 amendment to FLSA §7) |
| **De minimis doctrine — now narrow** | Historically allowed disregarding tiny, hard-to-record time fragments; now **narrow** given modern electronic timekeeping (*Troester v. Starbucks*, 2018). **California rejects it outright** for regularly recurring work. | A regularly recurring 5-minute post-clock-out closing task is compensable in CA even though it once might have been "de minimis." | Default `de_minimis_threshold_seconds` = **0** in CA. | 🔎 [*Troester v. Starbucks Corp.*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=Troester+v.+Starbucks) (Cal. 2018); [*Anderson v. Mt. Clemens Pottery Co.*](https://www.courtlistener.com/?q=Anderson+v.+Mt.+Clemens+Pottery) (1946) |

## 3. Overtime

*The federal floor is thin — only one premium, weekly-40 at 1.5×. Nearly all richer premiums (daily OT, double-time, 7th-day, penalty hours) are state, industry, or CBA overlays.*

### 3a. Onset / trigger

| Rule | Values | Worked example | Variants | Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Federal weekly OT — >40h/workweek** | 1.5× for every hour over **40** in a fixed, recurring **168-hour (7-day)** workweek. Each workweek stands alone — hours never average across two weeks. | Week 1 = 50h, Week 2 = 30h ≠ "80h straight." Week 1 alone contributes 10 OT hours. | The **workweek anchor** (start day/time, timezone) is per-employer-group config, not necessarily the calendar week. | [FLSA 29 U.S.C. §207(a)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/207) |
| **California daily OT — >8h/day, >12h/day** | **>8h and ≤12h** on days 1–6 of the workweek → **1.5×**. **>12h** → **2.0×**. Applies regardless of the weekly total. | A 14-hour Monday: 8h REG + 4h at 1.5× (hrs 8–12) + 2h at 2.0× (hrs >12). | Alternative Workweek Schedules shift the daily threshold to 10h or 12h (below). | [CA Labor Code §510](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=510); [IWC Wage Orders](https://www.dir.ca.gov/iwc/wageorderindustries.htm) |
| **Alaska daily OT — >8h/day** | **>8h/day** → **1.5×** for most employers with **≥4 employees**. | An Alaska retail employee working 10h in a day (employer has 6 staff) is owed OT on 2 hours regardless of the weekly total. | Threshold applies to covered employers only (small-employer carve-out below 4 employees). | 🔎 [AK Stat. §23.10.060](https://www.akleg.gov/basis/statutes.asp#23.10.060) |
| **Nevada daily OT — >8h/day, conditional** | **>8h/day** → **1.5×**, but **only** if the employee is paid **hourly** and earns **<1.5× the state minimum wage**. | An hourly Nevada worker earning below 1.5× state MW who works 9h in a day is owed 1 hour of daily OT; a higher-paid hourly worker at the same 9h is not. | Needs a per-worker eligibility flag (hourly + sub-1.5×-MW pay). | 🔎 [NV Rev. Stat. §608.018](https://www.leg.state.nv.us/nrs/NRS-608.html) |
| **Colorado daily/consecutive-hours OT — >12h** | **>12h/day OR >12 consecutive hours**, regardless of workday boundary, → **1.5×**. | A Colorado worker on a 22:00–11:00 shift (13 consecutive hours) triggers CO daily OT for the hour past 12 even though the shift spans two calendar workdays. | Distinct from the >12h/**day** trigger most states use — CO's is consecutive-hours based. | 🔎 [CO wage order (CDLE)](https://cdle.colorado.gov/wage-and-hour-law) |
| **Oregon manufacturing daily OT — >10h/day** | **>10h/day** in specific manufacturing sectors → **1.5×**. | A manufacturing worker in a covered Oregon facility working 11h in a day is owed 1 hour of daily OT. | Sector-specific; not a general Oregon daily-OT rule. | 🔎 [OR Rev. Stat. §653.261](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_653.261) |
| **7th-consecutive-day (CA)** | Working all **7 consecutive days within one workweek** (starting from workweek day 1) triggers: first **8h @1.5×**, remaining hours **@2.0×**, on that 7th day. | An employee working Tue–Mon where the workweek starts Sunday does **not** trigger 7th-day OT (Sunday — day 1 — wasn't worked, so only 6 consecutive days fall inside the workweek). | — | [CA Labor Code §510(a)](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=510) |
| **7th-consecutive-day (Kentucky)** | Working all 7 consecutive days of a workweek → **1.5×** for all hours that 7th day, with statutory exceptions. | A Kentucky worker working all 7 days of the workweek is paid 1.5× for the 7th day's hours, subject to the Act's exceptions. | Distinct statute, distinct exception list, from CA's 7th-day rule. | 🔎 [KY Rev. Stat. §337.050](https://apps.legislature.ky.gov/law/statutes/statute.aspx?id=52255) |
| **Massachusetts Sunday/holiday retail premium — fully repealed** | Massachusetts's mandatory Sunday/holiday retail premium (1.5×) was **fully repealed 1 Jan 2023**. | A Massachusetts retailer scheduling Sunday shifts in 2026 owes **no** state-mandated Sunday premium (unlike pre-2023). | — | 🔎 [MA Gen. Laws ch. 136](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXV/Chapter136) (repealed) |
| **Alternative Workweek Schedules (AWS) — CA** | A **2/3 secret-ballot vote** of the affected work unit adopts: **4/10** (four 10h days, no daily OT ≤10h), **9/80** (nine days over two workweeks = 80h, needs a workweek anchor bisecting the long day, typically at noon), or **3/12+4** (healthcare 12h shifts, Wage Orders 4 & 5). | Under a 4/10 schedule, a 10-hour Tuesday triggers **no** daily OT (vs. 2h of OT under the standard 8h threshold). | Once adopted, daily OT starts at the AWS threshold (10h or 12h); a non-regularly-scheduled day under AWS reverts to 1.5× from minute 1 to 8, 2.0× after. | [CA Labor Code §511](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=511) |
| **NY residential (live-in) — >44h/week** | Live-in domestic employees in New York earn OT only past **44 hours/week** (vs. the standard 40h). | A live-in caregiver working 46h/week is owed OT on 2 hours, not 6. | Federal live-in domestic workers are OT-**exempt** outright (§11.7 in the seed) — NY's 44h threshold is more generous than the federal exemption. | 🔎 [12 NYCRR §142-2.2](https://dol.ny.gov/labor-laws-and-regulations) |
| **NY farm worker — >52h/week (2026), phasing to 40h by 2032** | Farm-laborer OT onset in New York is **52 hours/week in 2026**, stepping down annually toward a **40-hour** threshold by **2032**. | A farm worker at 55h/week in 2026 is owed OT on 3 hours under the current 52h threshold. | Federal farm workers are OT-**exempt** outright; California and Washington also mandate ag OT (daily/weekly). | 🔎 [NY Farm Laborers Fair Labor Practices Act (2019)](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/LAB) + Wage Board schedule |
| **Hospital/residential-care 8/80 work period** | By **prior written agreement**, a **14-consecutive-day** period replaces the 7-day workweek. OT (1.5×) owed for hours **>8/workday OR >80/14-day period, whichever yields more** (no double-count — daily-OT hours already paid credit toward the 80-hour excess). | A nurse working 9h/day for 9 of 14 days (81h total, one 9h day) gets 1h of daily-OT-based premium; the period isn't independently retriggered for the same hour. | Election is per-employee, with an effective date; **cannot be applied retroactively**. | [FLSA §7(j) = 29 U.S.C. §207(j)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/29/207); [29 CFR §778.601](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/29/778.601) |
| **Public safety §7(k) work period** | Fire/police at a **public agency** may use a **7–28 consecutive-day** period.
